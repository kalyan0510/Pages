Satellites are very sensitive and costly equipment. Any failure in at least a single component of them can completely render the million dollar equipment useless. So all the failure prone equipment like onboard computers & orientation sensors are used in redundancy.

Even the redundancy does not make them infallible. Such is at the case for Astra-5A, a communication satellite launched in 1997 to a geo-stationary orbit. It suffered a orientation sensor (attitude sensor) failure and failed to keep its solar panels directed towards the sun. Consequently draining out of its battery resources and losing connection with the ground station.

In such scenarios there is nothing one can do without the orientation sensor data. This could be fatal for satellites orbiting in the LEO(Lower Earth Orbit), as they can quickly suffer from atmospheric drag and start falling to earth. But what if we can actually save these satellites from orientation sensor failures by literally looking at them to find their orientation?

We obviously cannot zoom our cameras at a satellite and see it. Even with a high-end telescopic equipment we cannot see them in the sky. If you have ever seen satellites in the night sky with your naked eyes, you might think about the possibility of capturing the satellite in a telescopic camera, but the problem here is that what you have seen is only the sun light reflected by shiny parts of satellite. Even if we capture, we might not have any usefull information to decode the satellite's orientation. Also, cameras at night needs high exposure time to capture the teeny tiny light reflected by the satellite. High exposure time will ruin the moving satellite's image. So we need a brighter source.

What else is brighter than our sun? So, can we capture the satellite when it is infront of the sun, that is, when the satellite is in between the camera and the sun. But with this we can only see the silhouette of the satellite behind the very bright sun. Because of the intense light from the sun, we can capture satellite images with extremely low exposure times. Also, perceptible images can be captured for large LEO satellites.

We can capture its dark silhouette and reverse calculate the possible orientations of the satellite and transform them in to relevant frame which the satellite uses for its attitude control (not that attitudeðŸ˜…).

Even in case the satellite has some unwanted spin, we can find the angular velocities using multiple photos. And fire the thrusters to compensate the spin and bring back to stable state.

Yaay! We saved a satellite.

Sun-and-Satellite-transit-predict

This code accurately predicts the time and location(lat|lon) of finding a satellite exactly between the viewer and the sun. We can find satellite's attitude(orientation) from its silhouette formed behind the bright sun.

Few terms: TLE (Two Line Elements): set of parameters which defines projected path/trajectory of a freely orbiting body. TLEs are short lived in case of LEO satellites.

Live updated TLEs of International Space station [Zarya] are maintained here: https://live.ariss.org/iss.txt

Usage

  1. Update the two line element in pathfinder.m
  2. run SatelliteTransitDataGenerator.m to generate the transit path.
  3. satellite_shadow_data.csv will contain time, latitude & longitude
  4. This file can be directly uploaded at https://www.google.com/maps to generate path

So if one sits at that spot and looks at the sun at that time, they can see the orbiting satellite's silhouette. And ofcourse good lens are needed.
